Entrepreneurship · BADM 261
2025
Problem Obsessed, Not Solution Obsessed

"Entrepreneurs should be problem obsessed rather than solution obsessed."

— BADM 261, University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign

I heard this quote in my BADM 261 class and honestly? It stopped me mid-note. I loved it the moment it was said. It was one of those things that just clicks — where you realize there's a name for something you've been doing wrong without even knowing it.

When I built Girls & Cycles, I started with the solution. I saw that women's health was underdiscussed, that period education was scattered, that there wasn't a resource that felt genuinely made for young women — and I just started building. A website. A tracker. A simulator. I kept adding features on top of features, all anchored to the same original idea I had.

But this quote made me take a step back and actually ask: what are all the problems here? Not just the ones I had already decided to solve, but all of them — the ones I hadn't noticed, the ones I had dismissed, the ones that felt too hard. So I started writing them down. Not mental notes anymore — an actual, growing list, organized by category: access, awareness, stigma, tracking gaps, health literacy, community, support systems.

What I found surprised me. Some problems I had already solved — just without framing them that way. I had built data visualizations to make cycle trends clearer. I had built a simulator to make the biology feel less abstract. Those were real solutions to real problems, and naming them that way made me see where I could go deeper: more categories, more features, more intentional design decisions.

But some of the problems on my list were bigger than what I could build right now. There are things I want to solve that I genuinely don't have the skillset for yet. And instead of feeling stuck by that, I've started approaching it differently — I'm thinking about which CS classes, which projects, which experiences will give me the tools I need. Data structures for smarter prediction models. ML for personalized health insights. Distributed systems for scale. The problems are teaching me what I need to learn next.

Being problem obsessed doesn't mean you stop building. It means you build with more intention — you start from a place of deep understanding instead of jumping straight to a solution that feels comfortable. That shift has genuinely changed how I think about every project I work on now.

Technology & Management · BADM 261
2025
Finding My Direction: Technology, Management, and Why I Want Both

"I was always interested in entrepreneurship — but I never had a name for the specific intersection I wanted to live in."

I first saw the email about the Hoeft Technology & Management Program over the summer, and I knew immediately it was something I wanted to explore. Growing up, both of my parents worked in technology and management — but I never really thought of that combination as its own sector or path. It was just their jobs. It wasn't until I got to UIUC and started taking BADM 261 that I started seeing it clearly: there's an entire world at the intersection of technical depth and strategic thinking, and that's exactly where I want to be.

One thing BADM 261 has really made me think about is the idea of sequencing — getting the technical foundation right before jumping into management or leadership. I've heard from so many people in class who went very technical first, spent years deep in the work, and then moved into management from a place of real credibility. That's inspired me. For the next two or three semesters, I want to do the same: stay in the technical weeds, work across as many different technologies and projects as I can, and build up genuine depth. Whether that's through my RSOs, internships, or personal projects — I want to earn the knowledge before I start pursuing higher-level roles.

BADM 261 has also opened my eyes to careers I genuinely hadn't considered before. I came in thinking tech and entrepreneurship were my two lanes. But this class showed me how many roles exist at that crossover — product, venture, consulting, operations, innovation strategy — and how differently people arrive at them. It expanded what I think my path could look like.

More than anything, I've realized that I don't just love entrepreneurship. I love technology and management together — the challenge of building something technically sound and then figuring out how to make it actually work in the world: how to scale it, how to communicate it, how to lead teams around it. BADM 261 didn't just introduce me to a program. It introduced me to a version of my future I hadn't quite been able to articulate before.
